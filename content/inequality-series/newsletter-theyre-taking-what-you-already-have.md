# They're Not Just Leaving You Behind. They're Taking What You Already Have.

You've heard the first half of this story. Young people can't buy houses. Graduates start their working lives £45,000 in debt. The pension your dad retired on doesn't exist anymore. House prices at seven times the average salary, when your parents bought at three.

That story is true. But it's only stage one. And if you're middle class — if you own a house, have a pension pot, maybe a bit in savings — you've probably been telling yourself that story is about *other people*. The young. The unlucky. Not you. You got in before the door closed.

Here's what I need you to understand: the door didn't close behind you. You're inside the machine too. Stage two is already happening, and it's happening to you.

## Stage one was locking out the young

Quick recap, because the mechanism matters. The ultra-wealthy cannot spend their money. There is no amount of consumption that drains a nine-figure fortune. So they invest it — in houses, in land, in companies, in anything that produces a return. That demand inflates asset prices. Gary Stevenson, who made his money as Citibank's most profitable trader betting that exactly this would happen, has been explaining it for years: when the rich get richer, they buy the assets ordinary people used to own, and the price of those assets rises out of reach of ordinary income.

This is why a nurse in 1985 could buy a house and a nurse in 2026 cannot. Not avocados. Not work ethic. Arithmetic.

But locking out the young was never the end state. It was the first harvest. The second harvest is the assets the middle class already holds.

## Stage two is happening to you right now

**Your pension is being farmed.** The shift from defined benefit to defined contribution wasn't a neutral modernisation — it transferred all investment risk from employers to you, and it put your retirement savings inside a fee machine. Annual management charges that look trivial — 1.5% — compound brutally. Over a 40-year working life, fees at that level consume roughly a third of your pot. The money doesn't vanish. It moves, every year, quietly, from your retirement to the institution managing it. You're not saving for retirement. You're paying rent on your own savings.

**Your house is already spoken for.** You think your home equity is your wealth, your security, your children's inheritance. Look at the social care settlement actually being constructed: the state will fund your care *after* your assets are gone. "Sell the house, then we'll talk." And who's waiting on the other side of that transaction? Private equity owns a major share of the UK care home sector, and it runs a recognisable playbook: buy the operator, extract management fees, sell the property and lease it back, load the company with debt, and when the structure collapses — administration, and on to the next one. Your house, the asset you spent thirty years paying for, is the feedstock.

**Your savings were deliberately eroded.** A decade of near-zero interest rates was not weather. It was policy, and its explicit purpose was to inflate asset prices. If you owned substantial assets, you got richer automatically — you didn't have to do anything. If your wealth was in a savings account because you couldn't afford to invest, it lost value in real terms, year after year. The same policy, two opposite outcomes, sorted entirely by how much you already had.

**Your business is being squeezed out.** Business rates that don't bend, compliance costs that hit a four-person firm as hard as a four-thousand-person one, and competition from operators with access to capital you will never have. The high street isn't dying of natural causes. Value is being transferred, incrementally, from independent operators to institutional ones.

**Your inheritance is being intercepted.** Inheritance tax thresholds frozen in nominal terms while asset prices inflate — a stealth expansion of the tax that never required a vote. Care costs consuming estates before they transfer. The middle-class promise — work hard, build something, pass it on — is being intercepted in transit. The wealth your parents built, and the wealth you're building, increasingly doesn't reach your children. It reaches a fund.

## How it's done

None of this is a conspiracy in a smoke-filled room. It doesn't need to be. It's structural, which is worse, because structures don't get embarrassed and stop.

Concentrated wealth must be invested. Investment at that scale needs assets to buy and income streams to capture. The remaining unclaimed assets and income streams in Britain are the ones the middle class holds: pension pots, home equity, savings, small businesses, inheritances. So the financial machinery — fees, leverage, sale-and-leaseback, rate policy, threshold freezes — is pointed at them. Not out of malice. Out of appetite. The money has to go somewhere, and you're what's left.

And here's the part Stevenson keeps repeating because nobody wants to hear it: **this does not self-correct.** Wealth concentration compounds. Every year the rich own more of the assets, collect more of the income from those assets, and use it to buy more of the assets. There is no natural floor. There is no point at which it stops on its own.

## The political story they're telling you

While this happens, you are being handed a story, and the story has three chapters.

**Chapter one: "intergenerational unfairness."** Boomers versus millennials. The 55-year-old homeowner versus the 25-year-old renter. It's a beautifully designed frame, because it points your anger *sideways and down* — at your parents, at your kids — and never up. The 55-year-old with a house and a pension and the 25-year-old with neither are not on opposite sides. They are at different stages of the same extraction. One is being locked out; the other is being stripped. The frame exists so they never compare notes.

**Chapter two: "capital flight."** Try to tax concentrated wealth and you're told the wealthy will simply leave. This functions as a permanent veto on reform — a threat that ends every conversation before it starts. Gabriel Zucman, who has spent his career tracing exactly where hidden wealth goes, is blunt about this: the technical problems are solved. Beneficial ownership registries work. Coordinated minimum taxation works — we built it for corporations. Exit taxes exist. What's missing is not a mechanism. It's a decision.

**Chapter three: "fiscal responsibility."** There's no money for social care, no money for housing, no money for the NHS — said with a straight face in a country holding trillions in private wealth, concentrated at the top more heavily every year. The money exists. The statement "there's no money" is only true if you've decided in advance that *that* money is untouchable.

Three chapters, one function: keep your eyes anywhere but on the balance sheet at the top.

## What actually needs to happen

Not vague redistribution. A specific instrument:

A progressive wealth tax on assets above £10 million. That touches well under 1% of the population — if your reaction was "but my house," it is not about you, and the people telling you it is are running chapter two on you. Globally coordinated, on the model Zucman has laid out, so the capital flight threat loses its teeth. Mandatory beneficial ownership transparency — no more wealth hiding behind shell companies and nominee directors. No trust carve-outs, no offshore carve-outs, because a wealth tax with carve-outs is a wealth tax on the insufficiently advised.

And the revenue pointed at the things being stripped: housing supply, social care that doesn't require liquidating your parents' home, education, the public services that used to be the middle class's second pension.

## The window

Here's the uncomfortable truth about timing. The middle class still has political weight precisely *because* it still has assets. Homeowners vote, donate, write to MPs, get listened to. Every year of extraction transfers a little more of that weight upward. A middle class with no equity, no savings, and no inheritance is just a larger working class with better memories — and considerably less leverage.

Which means this gets fixed while the middle class still has power, or it doesn't get fixed.

You are not an observer of this story. You're in it. The only question is whether you figure that out while it still matters.
