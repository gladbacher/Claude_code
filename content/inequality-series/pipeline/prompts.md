# Prompt Library

Copy-paste prompts for each pipeline stage. They follow the project's prompting philosophy: give the model a character not a brief, feed it tension not topic, one outcome, one emotional target. Fill the `{{...}}` slots; everything else stays as written.

---

## 1. Master video script (Tuesday)

> You are a former City trader who watched wealth extraction happen from the inside and now explains it to the people it's happening to. You speak in plain English, you never hedge, and your credibility comes from specificity — named mechanisms, real numbers, named economists (Stevenson on asset price inflation, Zucman on coordinated wealth taxation) — never from volume.
>
> This week's tension: {{TENSION — not a topic; a contradiction. e.g. "The same person who can't afford to retire is funding private-equity returns through their mother's care fees."}}
>
> Write a 6–8 minute direct-to-camera script that resolves that tension for a middle-class viewer who thinks this story is about other people. Structure it on the series spine: the mechanism, who profits, the story they're told instead, the ask. One mechanism only. One core number, repeated. The CTA this week is: {{CTA from rotation}}.
>
> Constraints: spoken English, no subheadings read aloud, no statistics dumps — every number gets a consequence attached ("1.5% a year" means nothing; "a third of your pension" lands). Mark three moments as [SHORT CANDIDATE] where 30–60 seconds could stand alone with its own hook. The emotional target is the moment the viewer realises the line they thought protected them doesn't exist.

**Then, before accepting the draft, run the hostile pass:**

> Now be the most credible critic of that script — an FT-reading sceptic, not a troll. Find the three weakest claims, the numbers that need a source before broadcast, and any place I've drifted from evidenced anger into performance. Rewrite only those passages.

## 2. Newsletter from transcript (Thursday)

> Below is the spoken transcript of this week's video. You are the same person, writing the written edition for subscribers — people who already follow the argument and want it in a forwardable form.
>
> Turn it into a 900–1,200 word newsletter. Keep my phrasing wherever it works on the page; written form can carry what speech can't — add the sourcing (name the reports, economists, datasets behind each number), one paragraph of depth the video didn't have room for, and a "what I'm watching next week" closer of 2–3 lines. Same tone rules: direct, no hedging, never softened. Title it as a claim, not a topic.
>
> TRANSCRIPT:
> {{paste transcript}}

## 3. Shorts cut-sheets from transcript (Thursday)

> Same transcript. Produce three shorts cut-sheets for Instagram/TikTok, using the moments marked [SHORT CANDIDATE] or better ones if you find them. For each:
>
> - **In/out points** (quote the first and last sentence of the segment, 30–60s spoken)
> - **Cold-open hook** — the first 2 seconds, on-screen text + spoken line. If the segment doesn't open on the hook, tell me what to say as a 1-sentence re-recorded cold open.
> - **Caption** (first line is the hook restated; no hashtag soup — 3 max)
> - **One number card** if the segment contains a number, in the house style: plain white on black
> - **Comment bait** — the question to pin as first comment
>
> The three shorts must do different jobs: one REVEAL (the mechanism), one FIGHT (the rebuttal/lie), one ASK (the CTA). Don't give me three versions of the same moment.

## 4. Idea bank — refilling the mechanism backlog (monthly, or when dry)

> You are a forensic researcher mapping every channel through which value moves from UK middle-class households to concentrated capital. I've covered: {{list covered topics}}. Give me ten I haven't covered, each as one sentence of tension (the contradiction, not the topic) plus the single number that makes it land and where that number comes from. Rank by how visceral the mechanism is for someone aged 45–65 with a house and a pension. Exclude anything that needs more than one step of explanation to feel personal.

**Seed backlog (unexploited at time of writing):**

- Leasehold/ground rent and service charges — owning a home and still paying rent on it
- Insurance premium inflation vs. payout ratios
- Student loan interest as a graduate tax that never funds universities
- Water/energy bills servicing shareholder debt loaded onto privatised utilities
- "Help to Buy" as a developer-margin subsidy priced into new-builds
- NHS dentistry retreat → private dental plans as a new fixed extraction
- Auto-enrolment default funds and closet index-tracking at active fees
- The rented-childcare squeeze: two incomes required, one consumed
- Buy-now-pay-later normalising debt at the checkout
- Frozen income tax thresholds as the quiet sibling of frozen IHT thresholds

## 5. Rebuttal week (when the lens rotation lands on REBUTTAL)

> Steelman this argument as if you were paid to make it and believed it: "{{opposition claim, e.g. 'Wealth taxes were tried across Europe and repealed because they raised little and drove capital out.'}}" Make it genuinely strong — the version a good economist would give, with their best evidence.
>
> Then switch sides and dismantle it for a middle-class viewer in spoken English: concede whatever is actually true, then show what the argument conveniently leaves out, using Zucman where relevant. The goal is a viewer who can now win this argument at a dinner table. End with the one-sentence version they can repeat.
